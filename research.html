Research

Building the next generation of high-power, ultra-stable, and intelligent fibre lasers.

The Fibre Photonics Lab develops the next generation of high-power fibre lasers and amplifiers, working at the intersection of physics, materials science and computation. Optical fibres are remarkably efficient, compact and robust light sources, but pushing them to higher powers introduces fundamental obstacles — waste heat, optical nonlinearities, and beam-quality degradation — that limit their stability and versatility. Our programme is built around three guiding principles: stability, by minimising power, frequency and nonlinear instabilities; versatility, by broadening laser performance across new spectral and temporal regimes; and intelligence, by enabling systems that dynamically adapt and optimise themselves in changing environments.

Rather than following the conventional reliance on single-mode fibres, we embrace multimode fibres and wavefront shaping as a richer platform for controlling light, combine anti-Stokes-fluorescence cooling to eliminate waste heat, and integrate programmable nonlinear materials such as liquid crystals to reach entirely new wavelengths and quantum states of light. The aim is to turn fibre lasers into ultra-stable, adaptable, high-performance light sources that open new frontiers in quantum computing, precision metrology and sensing, advanced manufacturing, and future communications.

01

Ultra-Stable High-Power Fibre Amplifiers

Scaling fibre lasers to high power usually generates waste heat that degrades both power and frequency stability. We develop heat-free, single-frequency fibre amplifiers using anti-Stokes-fluorescence (radiation-balanced) cooling, removing the quantum-defect heat at its source and the vibrations caused by external cooling. By engineering novel fibre structures such as air-clad microstructured fibres and purer, low-quenching glass compositions, we aim to push athermal amplifiers from the watt level toward hundreds of watts and beyond.

02

Nonlinear Optics in Multimode Fibres

Optical nonlinearities like stimulated Brillouin scattering and transverse mode instability have long capped how far fibre amplifiers can be power-scaled. We study why multimode fibres are surprisingly resilient to these instabilities, spreading light across many spatial modes to reduce intensity and weaken the feedback that drives nonlinear effects. This work overturns the long-held belief that multimode amplifiers can only produce poor beams, and underpins a route to high-power output with excellent beam quality.

03

Wavefront Shaping and "Smart" Amplifiers

By shaping the wavefront of a low-power seed laser, the complex amplification inside a multimode fibre can be steered to produce a clean single-mode beam, custom beam profiles, or tailored pulse shapes. We develop fast, physics-informed algorithms — including machine learning and nonlinear optimisation — that adapt in real time to correct for thermal and mechanical drift. A key aim is to replace bulky free-space optics with all-fibre, liquid-crystal-clad electro-optic wavefront shapers built directly into the fibre.

04

Novel Nonlinear Light Sources

Combining stable fibre lasers with engineered nonlinear materials lets us reach wavelengths and quantum states of light that fibres alone cannot produce. A particular focus is on recently discovered ferroelectric nematic liquid crystals, which offer strong second-order nonlinearity together with electro-optic tunability. Target applications include stable watt-level ultraviolet lasers for neutral-atom quantum computing and high-power squeezed-light sources for quantum sensing and gravitational-wave detection.

05

Specialty Optical Fibres and Materials

Achieving heat-free, high-power operation depends on the fibre itself. We design and characterise specialty fibres and glasses — including new Yb-doped silicate compositions that have set records for the largest optical cooling and lowest quenching. This research links careful measurement of cooling performance, using high-resolution slow-light fibre-Bragg-grating sensors, to the design of fibres that outperform the best commercial options.

06

Transparent Reconfigurable Intelligent Surfaces

As wireless communications move to ever higher frequencies, signals are increasingly scattered and attenuated by everyday objects. We bring concepts from optical wavefront shaping to the radio-frequency domain, developing reconfigurable intelligent surfaces that reshape how signals propagate. The aim is transparent, liquid-crystal-clad antenna surfaces that overcome the limitations of opaque designs and integrate seamlessly into real-world environments for 6G and beyond.
